History of Chemistry

Ancient Chemistry

Alchemy: A protoscience practiced in Arabia, Greece, Egypt, and China. Alchemists sought to turn base metals into gold and cure diseases, and though unsuccessful, they pioneered techniques for purifying and analyzing substances.

Early Metallurgy: Civilizations developed methods of extracting metals and creating alloys like bronze and advanced steels (e.g., India’s wootz steel).

Contributions from Ancient Civilizations: Fire control led to glass discovery and metal purification. Egyptians mastered embalming and metal manipulation.

Ancient Era

The Scientific Revolution

Robert Boyle: Founder of modern chemistry. Introduced Boyle’s Law and emphasized quantitative experiments.

Antoine Lavoisier: Father of modern chemistry. Established the law of conservation of mass, named oxygen and hydrogen, and contributed to the metric system.

Scientific Method: Marked a shift toward empirical, systematic experimentation and theoretical frameworks.

17th–18th Century

Modern Chemistry

Dalton’s Atomic Theory: Proposed indivisible atoms, identical per element, rearranging in reactions.

Mendeleev’s Periodic Table: Organized elements by atomic mass and properties in 1869, predicting undiscovered elements.

Quantum Chemistry: Emerged after the discovery of the electron in 1897. Applied quantum mechanics to explain bonding and molecular properties.

19th–20th Century

Notable Chemists

Marie Curie: Discovered polonium and radium, pioneered radioactivity research, first woman to win a Nobel Prize.

Robert Boyle: Bridged alchemy and modern chemistry with his laws of gases.

Antoine Lavoisier: Revolutionized chemistry with systematic naming and conservation of mass.

John Dalton: Introduced atomic theory, explaining atomic behavior by weight.

Ionic Bonds

Ionic bonds are formed through the transfer of electrons between atoms, typically between a metal and a nonmetal.

Process:
1. Electron Transfer: One atom (usually a metal) loses electrons to become a positively charged ion (cation).
2. Ion Formation: Another atom (usually a nonmetal) gains electrons to become a negatively charged ion (anion).
3. Electrostatic Attraction: The oppositely charged ions are attracted to each other, forming the ionic bond.
Properties: High melting/boiling points, hard and brittle, conduct electricity in solution.

×

Covalent Bonds

Covalent bonds are formed through the sharing of electrons between atoms, usually between nonmetals.

Types:
Single Bond: One pair of electrons is shared.
Double Bond: Two pairs of electrons are shared.
Triple Bond: Three pairs of electrons are shared.
Polarity:
Nonpolar Covalent Bond: Electrons are shared equally between atoms
Polar Covalent Bond: Electrons are shared unequally, resulting in a partial positive (δ+) and partial negative (δ-) charge on the atoms
Properties: Lower melting points, may be gas/liquid/solid, poor conductors.

×

Metallic Bonds

Metallic bonds are formed between metal atoms, where electrons are delocalized and free to move throughout the metal structure.
Electron Sea Model:
Metal atoms release their valence electrons into a "sea" of electrons, which are not associated with any particular atom.
Properties: Conductive, malleable, ductile, shiny.

Phase Change

Phase changes involve the transition of matter from one state to another.
Common phase changes include:
Melting: Solid to liquid (e.g., ice melting into water).
Freezing: Liquid to solid (e.g., water freezing into ice).
Boiling (Vaporization): Liquid to gas (e.g., water boiling into steam).
Condensation: Gas to liquid (e.g., steam condensing into water).
Sublimation: Solid to gas (e.g., dry ice sublimating into carbon dioxide gas).
Deposition: Gas to solid (e.g., frost forming on a cold surface).

Solid

Definition: Solids have a fixed shape and volume.
Molecular Arrangement: Molecules are tightly packed in a fixed arrangement.
Properties: Rigid, incompressible.
Examples: Ice, rock, wood, metal.

×

Liquid

Definition: Liquids have fixed volume but take the shape of their container.
Molecular Arrangement: Molecules are closely packed but can move.
Properties: Fluid, nearly incompressible.
Examples: Water, oil, alcohol.

×

Gas

Definition: Gases have no fixed shape or volume, expand to fill container.
Molecular Arrangement: Molecules widely dispersed, moving randomly.
Properties: Compressible, low density.
Examples: Air, oxygen, helium.

×

Plasma

Definition: Plasma is an ionized gas with positive ions and free electrons.
Molecular Arrangement: High energy causes electrons to strip from atoms.
Properties: Conducts electricity, influenced by magnetic fields.
Examples: Lightning, stars, neon signs.

×

Organic Chemistry

Definition: Study of carbon-containing compounds and their properties.
Key Concepts: Carbon bonding, isomers.
Functional Groups: Determine characteristic chemical reactions.
Reactions: Addition, elimination, substitution, rearrangement.
Applications: Pharmaceuticals, polymers, fuels.

×

Inorganic Chemistry

Definition: Study of compounds not primarily based on carbon-hydrogen bonds.
Includes: Metals, salts, minerals, coordination compounds.
Types: Acids, bases, salts, oxides, intermetallics.
Applications: Materials, catalysts, pigments.

×

Physical Chemistry

Definition: Uses physics principles to explain chemical systems.
Branches:
Thermodynamics – energy, enthalpy, entropy, Gibbs free energy.
Kinetics – reaction rates, mechanisms, catalysts.
Electrochemistry – electricity + chemical change (batteries, electrolysis).
Quantum Chemistry – bonding, molecular orbitals, electron behavior.
Purpose: Predicting and explaining chemical behavior.

×

Biochemistry

Definition: Study of chemical processes in living organisms.
Macromolecules:
Proteins – enzymes, hormones, structural roles.
Carbohydrates – energy storage, structure, signaling.
Lipids – membranes, hormones, insulation, energy.
Nucleic Acids – DNA & RNA, genetic storage/expression.
Metabolism: Catabolism (breakdown), Anabolism (synthesis), pathways like glycolysis & Krebs cycle.

×

Analytical Chemistry

Definition: Focuses on separation, identification, and quantification of matter.
Qualitative Analysis: Identifies components in a sample.
Quantitative Analysis: Determines how much of each component is present.
Applications: Environmental monitoring, pharmaceuticals, food safety, clinical labs.
